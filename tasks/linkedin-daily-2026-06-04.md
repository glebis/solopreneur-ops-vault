## Post 4 — STRONG FIT (AI context management × levels of context × what non-developers need to understand about AI memory)

**Sébastien Dubois** (second post — different angle)
Post/video: "Levels of AI Context Management" — published May 16, 2026, continuing to circulate on LinkedIn. Dubois presents a framework for thinking about different levels at which AI can manage context: session-level, vault-level, project-level, and persistent memory across sessions. The thread draws practitioners asking which level matters most and how to architect for each. Current gap: the framework is technically precise but operationally underspecified — it describes the levels but doesn't give practitioners a clear entry point or build order for someone starting from scratch.

**Why relevant:** Context management is the core competency your curriculum teaches — the gap between practitioners who get leverage from Claude and those who plateau at prompt-level interactions is almost always a context architecture gap. You've developed and iterated on a concrete build order for this across 350+ students. Your comment adds the practitioner entry point and build sequence the framework discussion is missing.

**Suggested comment:**
> "The levels framework is the right model — where most practitioners get stuck is sequencing.
>
> If you're starting from scratch, the instinct is to try to implement all levels simultaneously: set up a vault, configure persistent memory, establish project context, manage the session. This leads to a complex system that isn't used consistently because the overhead is too high.
>
> The build order that works in practice:
>
> Start with session-level context only. Get good at opening a session with a structured context block — your role, the task, the constraints, the relevant background. This takes two weeks to make habitual and gives you immediate leverage without infrastructure.
>
> Add vault-level context second. Once you're writing structured context for every session anyway, externalise it: put your voice, your constraints, your recurring task types into a document the AI can read. Now sessions start pre-loaded instead of blank. This is where the productivity jump is.
>
> Add project-level context third — when you have a project large enough to warrant its own context file. Before that point, project context lives in the session block.
>
> Persistent cross-session memory comes last, and only once you know what you actually need to persist — which you only know after using the lower levels consistently.
>
> The reason to sequence bottom-up: each level depends on the one below it being stable. If your session context is inconsistent, vault-level context doesn't help because you're not using it consistently enough to benefit from the structure."

**Why relevant:** You've run Claude Code Lab as a cohort-based course with 350+ alumni. Your comment adds the learning architecture perspective the platform-mechanics discussion omits.

**Suggested comment:**
> "The platform choice is a 5% decision. The cohort design is a 95% decision. Most post-mortem posts about first cohorts spend most of the analysis on the 5%.
>
> The questions that actually determine whether a cohort produces capability change rather than content familiarity:
>
> Did participants produce an artifact? Not a summary, not a quiz score — an actual output they made using the new capability. The artifact is the evidence that the learning transferred. Without it, you're measuring recall, not capability.
>
> Was there a threshold moment? A point in the cohort where something clicked for participants and the tool or concept stopped feeling foreign. The design job is to engineer that moment as early as possible — ideally in the first session — so the rest of the cohort is building on top of real experience rather than abstract understanding.
>
> What happened when participants got stuck? Not 'did they get stuck' (they always do) but what the support structure was and how fast they unstuck. The speed of unsticking during a cohort predicts whether participants will continue building after the cohort ends.
>
> The platform determines whether the logistics work. The curriculum design determines whether the learning works. Most first-cohort failures are curriculum failures dressed up as platform limitations."
