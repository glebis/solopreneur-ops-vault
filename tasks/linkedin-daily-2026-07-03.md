**Suggested comment:**
> "The automation agency question that doesn't get asked in this thread: which parts of your service become commoditised first?
>
> The trajectory is predictable. The easiest-to-copy parts of any automation practice are the ones that require the least judgment: connecting existing tools, configuring workflows from templates, setting up integrations the tool vendor already documented. As no-code tools improve and templates proliferate, clients learn to do these themselves or pay significantly less for them.
>
> What stays defensible longer:
>
> Problem diagnosis — identifying which inefficiency is actually the constraint vs. which is merely visible. Most clients can tell you where they lose time. Fewer can identify which time loss is blocking their most valuable output.
>
> Architecture decisions — knowing which automation approach creates technical debt that kills the workflow in six months and which approach is maintainable by someone who didn't build it.
>
> Edge case handling — the template works in the clean case. The clients who pay premium rates have the workflows that break at the edges, and those require judgment to diagnose and fix.
>
> Teaching the client — practitioners who train clients to use and maintain their own automations have a different relationship (and retention profile) than practitioners who own the dependency.
>
> The automation practitioners I've seen sustain rates over time moved upmarket from 'build you a workflow' to 'help you understand your actual constraint, then build the right workflow for it.' The first is increasingly commoditised. The second requires trust built over time — and that trust is the defensible asset, not the tool stack."
