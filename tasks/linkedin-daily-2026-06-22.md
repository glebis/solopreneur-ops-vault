## Post 3 — STRONG FIT (AI Agent vs Agentic AI vocabulary × why the distinction determines what learners try to build)

**Greg Coquillo** — AI Infrastructure Product Leader | Scaling GPU Clusters for Frontier Models | Microsoft Azure AI & HPC | LinkedIn Top Voice; posts about AI architecture, agentic systems, and AI skill development; large audience of developers, ML practitioners, and AI-curious professionals (linkedin.com/in/greg-coquillo).

Post: "AI Agent vs Agentic AI: Most people use the terms interchangeably" — the post makes a case for definitional clarity: AI agents are discrete units with defined scope; agentic AI is a behavior pattern describing autonomous, multi-step, adaptive completion. Most practitioners conflate them, which creates architecture-level mistakes when building systems. Thread: practitioners debating whether the distinction is meaningful or marketing terminology; developers sharing examples; some arguing definitions don't matter if the system works. Current gap: the debate is happening at the architectural and definitional level, but the real cost of conflation shows up a layer below — at the learning and design level. What model you learn first shapes what you try to build.

**Why relevant:** You teach vocabulary as infrastructure. The AI agent vs. agentic AI distinction isn't primarily an architecture debate for your audience — it's a curriculum sequencing question. Which mental model you introduce first determines whether learners go looking for the right tool or go designing the right task scope. That's a teachable distinction with direct pedagogical consequences.

**Suggested comment:**
> "The definitions debate is real, but the practical cost of conflation usually shows up below the architecture level.
>
> Practitioners who learn 'AI agent' as their foundational mental model tend to adopt a product metaphor: there's a thing, it does a thing, I need to find the right thing. They optimize for tool selection.
>
> Practitioners who learn 'agentic AI' as their foundation tend to adopt a behavior metaphor: there's a capability that expresses differently depending on context. They optimize for task scope design.
>
> These are different starting questions and they lead to different first projects. 'Which agent should I use?' vs. 'what's the right scope for the task I want to automate?' — one leads to tool shopping, the other leads to workflow design.
>
> The people who advance fastest usually hit an inflection point where the product metaphor breaks down and the behavior metaphor clicks in. That transition is teachable and documentable — it doesn't have to happen through accumulated frustration.
>
> For architects: the agent/agentic distinction matters at the system design level. For educators and practitioners: it matters at the vocabulary sequencing level — which model do you introduce first, and which do you use as the reframe when the first one limits thinking?"

**Post URL:** [Greg Coquillo — AI Agent vs Agentic AI: Key Differences](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/greg-coquillo_ai-agent-vs-agentic-ai-most-people-use-the-activity-7432076706095517696-tASm) — the teaching-level vocabulary consequence is entirely absent from this thread; the "product metaphor vs. behavior metaphor" framing reframes the debate for practitioners building mental models rather than systems; **distinct from June 16's Greg Coquillo post about no-code vs coded agents — that was architecture, this is pedagogy.** Verify recency before commenting.

**Suggested comment:**
> "Cohort format for AI engineering skills has a specific advantage that rarely appears in the self-paced vs. cohort debate: it makes semantic failure visible in real time.
>
> When you write bad SQL, you get an error. When you write a subtly flawed agent workflow, the system completes successfully — and produces output that looks plausible but is wrong in a way that might take three runs to catch.
>
> In a cohort, when someone watches your Claude output and says 'wait — why did it skip that validation step?' you catch a semantic error immediately. That feedback is qualitatively different from a forum post after the fact. The error-catching speed is different. The diagnostic specificity is different.
>
> This is why AI engineering benefits from cohort learning more than most technical disciplines: the failure mode is ambiguous rather than explicit, and peer observation compresses the feedback loop that the ambiguity otherwise stretches out.
>
> The curriculum design variable that determines whether the cohort format actually delivers this: does the course require each participant to ship a deployable artifact — something that runs correctly without them watching it — or does it stop at conceptual understanding? Cohorts that produce autonomous outputs create different alumni intuitions than cohorts that produce AI literacy.
>
> 'Learn by doing' gets at this. The specific test: after the cohort, can participants build something new they haven't seen in the curriculum, without help?"
