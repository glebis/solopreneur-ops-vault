## Post 2 — PERFECT FIT (workflow automation × beyond trigger logic × skill architecture)

**Felicia Thomson** — Enterprise AI strategist
"AI Workflow Automation: Beyond Rigid Triggers" — Post arguing that the dominant workflow automation paradigm (if X then Y, trigger-based pipelines) is hitting a wall because real work is full of judgment calls that triggers can't encode. The frame: agents succeed when they handle *situations*, not when they handle *conditions*. Thread drawing in ops leads and AI practitioners debating how to express judgment as structure without over-engineering it.

**Why relevant:** "Beyond triggers" is the exact architectural shift your Claude Code Lab curriculum teaches. The practitioner insight you have from 50+ skills: the skills that outlast trigger-based automation are the ones that encode the judgment as a question the agent asks the human at the right moment — not a branching condition the designer tries to pre-map. That's the design principle the thread doesn't have yet.

**Suggested comment:**
> "The trigger-vs-judgment gap is real, and the practical path through it is narrower than most architecture discussions suggest. In skills that survive real use (vs. demos), the pattern is: encode the routine as structure, but surface the exception to a human rather than trying to pre-condition for it. 'If the email is from a VIP, do X, else do Y' — that's a trigger, and it breaks the moment a new category of VIP appears. 'Draft a reply, and if you're uncertain whether this person needs immediate attention, flag it and ask before sending' — that's a judgment-bearing structure that grows with use rather than accumulating edge-case branches. The practical heuristic I give practitioners: wherever you'd write a branching condition, ask whether a senior colleague in that role would just *know* the answer or would actually *pause and ask someone*. If they'd pause, you want a human confirmation gate, not a branch. That's the zone where agents outperform triggers."
