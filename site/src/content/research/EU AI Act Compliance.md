# EU AI Act: What a Solopreneur AI Educator in Berlin Needs to Know (2026)

> Regulation creates anxiety. Anxiety creates demand. Demand creates revenue — if you're the one who can explain it.

## EU AI Act Status — What's in Effect Right Now

The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) is rolling out in phases. Here's what applies as of April 2026:

### Already in Effect

| Date | What Became Applicable |
|------|----------------------|
| **2 Feb 2025** | Prohibited AI practices (social scoring, real-time biometric mass surveillance, manipulation of vulnerable groups) |
| **2 Feb 2025** | **AI Literacy obligation (Article 4)** — all organizations using AI must ensure staff have adequate AI literacy |
| **2 Aug 2025** | General-Purpose AI (GPAI) model rules — transparency, documentation, copyright compliance for model providers |
| **2 Aug 2025** | EU AI Office governance structures operational |

### Coming Next

| Date | What Applies |
|------|-------------|
| **2 Aug 2026** | **The big one:** High-risk AI system obligations — risk management, data governance, technical documentation, human oversight, accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity |
| **2 Aug 2026** | Transparency obligations for all AI systems interacting with people (chatbots, content generation) |
| **2 Aug 2026** | National market surveillance authorities begin enforcement |
| **Dec 2027** | Possible delayed deadline for high-risk systems (European Parliament voted to push back; requires Council agreement by June 2026) |
| **2 Aug 2027** | GPAI models placed on market before Aug 2025 must comply |
| **31 Dec 2030** | AI systems in large-scale EU IT systems must comply |

**Key uncertainty:** The European Parliament voted to delay high-risk AI system compliance to December 2027, but this requires a political agreement in the Council before June 2026. Until confirmed, treat August 2026 as the operative deadline.

## Impact on AI Educators

### The AI Literacy Mandate — Your Biggest Opportunity

**Article 4** is already in effect and directly creates demand for your services:

> Organizations must ensure that their staff — and individuals acting on their behalf, including freelancers — have been furnished with adequate AI literacy when using or operating AI systems.

This means:
- **Every company using AI tools needs training** — not optional, legally required
- **No specific certification mandated** — organizations have flexibility in how they implement AI literacy, which means independent educators can serve this market
- **Proportional to risk** — higher-risk AI use requires more comprehensive training
- **Ongoing obligation** — not a one-time checkbox, must be maintained as tools evolve

### Do Your Courses Need Compliance?

As an AI educator, you primarily **deploy** AI systems (using Claude Code, MCP) rather than **provide** them (you don't build the underlying models). Your compliance position:

| Activity | Your Role | Obligation |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Teaching with Claude Code | Deployer | AI literacy for yourself and participants (Article 4) — already covered by the nature of the lab |
| Building skills/agents for clients | Deployer/potentially Provider | Depends on what the agent does — if high-risk (HR, credit, education assessment), additional obligations |
| Running MCP servers | Deployer | Transparency if the system interacts with end users |
| Publishing vault/site | Content creator | Label AI-generated content (transparency obligation) |

### Tools That Generate Code/Content

Claude Code and similar tools are classified as **General-Purpose AI** systems. The compliance burden falls primarily on the **provider** (Anthropic), not on you as a deployer. However:
- You must inform end users when they're interacting with AI-generated content
- If you build agents that make decisions affecting people (hiring, grading, access), those could be high-risk

## Impact on Solopreneurs Using AI

### Freiberufler in Berlin — Your Specific Situation

As a self-employed individual (Freiberufler/Kleinunternehmer) in [[research/Berlin Solopreneur 2026|Berlin]], you are classified as a **microenterprise** under the Act (fewer than 10 employees, under €2M turnover). Key implications:

**No blanket exemption.** SMEs are not exempt from the AI Act. Every entity engaged in economic activity — including self-employed individuals — has obligations. However:

- **Proportionate enforcement** — supervisory authorities must account for organizational size and resources
- **Lower fines** — maximum penalties are proportionate to company size
- **Priority sandbox access** — SMEs get free priority access to regulatory sandboxes
- **Simplified documentation** — the Commission is developing simplified technical documentation forms for SMEs

### What You Must Do

1. **AI Literacy (NOW)** — Document that you and your team/contractors have adequate AI knowledge. Your lab curriculum essentially does this.
2. **Transparency (Aug 2026)** — Label AI-generated content. If agents interact with users, disclose that they're AI.
3. **Risk Assessment** — If you build agents that process personal data or make consequential decisions, assess the risk level.
4. **Documentation** — Maintain records of which AI systems you use, for what purposes, and what safeguards exist.

### What You DON'T Need to Worry About

- You don't need to register AI systems (only high-risk providers do)
- You don't need a conformity assessment (that's for high-risk AI providers)
- You don't need to audit Claude Code's training data (that's Anthropic's obligation)
- You don't need certification for your courses (no mandatory certification exists)

## GDPR + AI Act Intersection

### The Double Compliance Problem

When AI agents process personal data (which they almost always do), you trigger **both** regulatory frameworks:

| Requirement | GDPR | AI Act |
|-------------|------|--------|
| Impact assessment | DPIA (Article 35) | FRIA (Article 27) — high-risk only |
| Documentation | Records of processing | Technical documentation |
| Transparency | Privacy notice | AI disclosure |
| Data minimization | Collect minimum needed | BUT need representative data for bias prevention |
| Human oversight | Right to human review of automated decisions | Mandatory for high-risk systems |

### The Tension

The AI Act requires **representative datasets** to prevent bias (you need demographic data to ensure fairness). GDPR requires **data minimization** (collect only what's necessary). This creates a genuine compliance tension that organizations struggle with.

**Teaching angle:** This is exactly the kind of nuance that makes a workshop valuable. Generic AI courses skip this; your lab can address it practically.

### Practical Implications for [[frameworks/MCP for Solopreneurs|MCP Workflows]]

When your agents connect to external services via MCP:
- **Data flows through the agent** — every MCP call potentially processes personal data
- **Local processing preferred** — tools that run locally (Obsidian MCP, file system) have lower GDPR risk than cloud services
- **Audit trail needed** — log what data agents access, even for personal use, as a best practice
- **73% vulnerability rate** — audits found that 73% of enterprise AI agent implementations in 2024 had some GDPR compliance vulnerability
